Motion for a Resolution by the Committee on Civil Liberties, Justice and Home Affairs I (LIBE I)

Face not recognised: While the EU’s 2021 Aritificial Inteligence (AI) Act aims to regulate AI technology, human rights groups say that it is leaving citizens vulnerable, for example to discrimination. How can the EU become ready for the digital age, while upholding European values and fundamental rights?


Submitted by: Samuel Akinsulire (Tullamore College, IE), Patrick Delany (St Michael’s College, IE), Angelos Eleftheriou (St Vincent’s Glasnevin, IE), Brownyn Fleming (Coláiste Cholmcille Ballyshannon, IE), Layla Hannafin (Mercy Mounthawk Tralee, IE), Adam Kenny (Marist College Athlone, IE), Rian Mulcahy (St Francis College Rochestown, IE), Clara O'Dowd (Mercy Mounthawk Tralee, IE) David O’Shea (Marist College Athlone, IE), Giorgia Roncarolo (IT) Georgina Thornton (St Mary’s Secondary School Mallow, IE) , Anastasia Valakoudi (Scoil Mhuire Cork, IE), Emily Vrahimi (Chairperson, CY)


The European Youth Parliament aims to ensure the safe and reasonable regulation of Artificial Intelligence (AI) technology. We wish to encourage innovation and growth in the field whilst respecting European fundamental rights, and allowing room for flexibility of the legislation.

Because

  • The proposed Artificial Intelligence (AI) Act, although a significant step towards harmonising AI regulation, poses risks of overregulation through its guidelines, which could discourage investment,
  • Insufficient investment in AI technology by the EU hinders the current and potential innovation and development in the field,
  • Individuals are inadequately prepared for the digital age, with nearly half of Europeans lacking basic digital skills and knowledge in 2019,
  • AI development risks jobs security and mobility, with an estimation of 218 million unskilled jobs and 107 million skilled jobs being affected by 2030,
  • Input of biased data sets, resulting from a history of discrimination and lack of diversity in research teams in AI technology can lead the development of discriminatory AI technology,
  • Remote biometric recognition technology, such as facial recognition, along with data breaches, can enable the state to systematically identify and track individuals, threatening their rights to privacy, liberty, and security,
  • The storage of data and the use of AI as a forecasting tool to e.g. predict the user’s political affiliations may threaten an individual’s right to freedom of expression and thought of conscience and religion;

The European Youth Parliament, to achieve these aims,

  1. Recommends the European Commission to amend the AI Act by:
    • reducing the non-compliance fines for companies established within the past two years or falling under a certain income band selling or producing high-risk AI,
    • re-evaluating the definition of and guidelines on AI based on feedback from experts in the field, recruited into the European AI Board;
  2. Asks the European Commission to amend the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) to:
    • clarify the types of biometric data that can be stored,
    • lay down a maximum amount of time the data can be stored without renewal of permission of the individual enforced by the Data Protection Officer (DPO);
  3. Designates the European Patent Office (EPO) to require that AI developers disclose any biases in their software such as gender or racial bias;
  4. Requests the European Personnel Selection Office (EPSO) implement guidelines for the minimum human employee quota per company in accordance with size and scale to combat dehumanisation of the workplace;
  5. Urges Member States to implement a scheme similar to the Pandemic Unemployment Payment (PUP) to financially support those who have lost their job due to AI development;
  6. Encourages Member States to implement a course on AI technology in public schools, based on the blueprint of the free and online AI courses of the University of Helsinki;
  7. Invites the Panel for the Future of Science and Technology of the European Parliament (STOA) to facilitate an online outreach programme to educate people on AI technologies;
  8. Endorses the expansion of the Coordinated Plan on AI by:
    • encouraging Austria, Belgium, Croatia, Greece, Ireland, Italy, Romania, and Slovenia to publish their national plan,
    • recommending the expansion of the Horizon Europe and Digital Europe investment schemes;
  9. Further requests the European Commission to amend the Erasmus+ programme guide for the future years and include the focus on AI courses in third level education in the relevant call;
  10. Further encourages the European Commission and Member States to allocate funds from the European Structural and Investment Funds programme to AI developers and AI small and medium enterprises (SME’s).
