Committee on Constitutional Affairs (AFCO)

The kids are (not) alright: While young Europeans are politically engaging for ends like Fridays for Future or Black Lives Matter, the minimum age to vote - a basic aspect of political participation - remains 18 in almost all Member States. Considering that the European Parliament already recommended lowering the voting age in 2015, how can the EU finally enhance these rights for young people?


By Éabha Murphy (IE)

Abstract

At the European Parliament elections in 2019, voter turnout reached a record high of 50.6%. This was largely attributed to the increased participation of young people, with the number of voters under the age of 25 having increased by 14 percent from the previous election. For the majority of Member States, those eligible to vote were above the age of 18. This excludes a key sector of society who have been engaging in civil and political participation activities. While young people involve themselves in activism, they are unable to access the most basic civic duty that is voting. This creates an imbalance in the voices which are heard by politicians as they are more likely to prioritise the issues of those who got them elected.


The popular justification for lowering the voting age is engagement in other civic duties, such as paying taxes: as youngsters can be employed, but cannot vote, this can be seen as taxation without representation. Additionally, the concept of voting power comes into play as young people are traditionally underrepresented in government, due to low voter turnout among the young demographic. The over-65-year-old demographic has been overrepresented, due to high voter turnout. Offering more young people the opportunity to vote has been shown to decrease this gap between these two groups. Additionally, the argument has been made that young people will face the consequences of a vote for a longer period of time. For example during the Brexit referendum, 75% of young people voted to remain in the EU, yet they were overpowered by the older generations who voted to leave.

Keywords

  • Age of majority: The age at which someone is legally considered an adult. In European countries this is typically 18, however it is 16 in Scotland unlike the rest of the UK.
  • Suffrage: The right to vote in political elections.
  • Political Participation: The activities of citizens that attempt to influence the structure of government, the selection of government officials, or the policies of the government. Examples include signing petitions, voting in elections, participating in interest groups, running for office, protesting, and emailing government officials.
  • Minimum voting age: The minimum age established by law that a person must be before they become eligible to vote in a public election. Most commonly this is 18.

Key Actors and Stakeholders

Member States: It is the Member States who determine the voting ages in their local and national elections and referendums. The EU is not within competence to force Member States to lower the minimum voting age. Instead, it is upon the Member States to implement such a constitutional change. Certain European political parties such as the Greens as well as individual politicians have been lobbying to lower the voting ages across the EU to ensure and strengthen the youth vote.


European Commission: The European Commission is the legislative branch of the EU and is able to propose legislation to lower the minimum voting age across European elections. They would first have to assess the possible social and economic consequences of creating such a policy before submitting the proposal to the European Parliament and the Council of the EU for review, debate and voting.


Young People: Young people are the key stakeholder and are most affected by this issue as they are limited in the ways by which they can advocate for this change in voting law. Some key organisations that represent youth interests such as the European Youth Forum and its associated councils and organisations actively lobby the Member States’ governments on behalf of the youth who are ineligible to vote. Young people regularly display their desire to partake in political discourse through their activism such as leading movements like Fridays for Future,but cannot fully enforce change as they cannot choose their own parliamentary representation.

Relevant Policies and Existing Measures

While the majority of the EU has set the minimum voting age at 18 this has some exceptions. Austria was the first Member State to introduce teen suffrage in 2007, allowing them to vote in national elections. Moreover, 16 and 17-year-old Austrian voters are able to vote in European elections. Teen suffrage has been for the most part successful in Austria: while the 16-17-year-old demographic was less represented in the polls than those aged 30 and over, it does have a bigger turnout than the 18-21-year-olds. This has been supported by education policy measures such as mandatory civic education classes to promote political maturity. Other countries such as Greece, Malta, and Belgium have followed in Austria’s footsteps. Greece has given 17-year-olds the right to vote in European elections and Belgium has introduced a bill that allows 16-year-olds to vote in European elections in 2024.


In 2015, the European Parliament recommended Member States to lower the voting age to 16. While some states followed the recommendation, such as the ones mentioned above, the majority did not lower its voting age.


In other Member States such as Estonia and some of Germany’s federal states, the voting age has been lowered to 16 for local elections. This has been argued to be an effective measure as it guarantees limited teenage suffrage and the action of casting a vote during your formative years has shown to be essential in ensuring voter participation in your later years. While it is not total enfranchisement, it has been effective in creating this “habit” of voting. During the 2014 Scottish Independence Referendum, 75% of registered 16- and 17-year-olds turned out to vote following a once-off allowance for them to participate. The UK “Vote at 16” coalition campaign has been lobbying since 1998 to ensure this right for young people through their vast network of supportive politicians and organisations. From this one off they were able to further campaign and in 2015 a bill ensuring voting rights to 16 year olds was passed in the Scottish Parliament.

Key Challenges

One of the major challenges to lowering the minimum age of voting is the strong opposition it faces. Many simply believe 16-year-olds are too young, impulsive, lacking political knowledge and too easily influenced to be allowed to vote. Many believe that these teenagers are too idealistic and do not have enough life experience to be able to make sound political decisions about their stance on tax, housing, the economy, etc. Seeing as humans do not fully mature until the age of 25 (when their brains have fully developed) the case has also been made to instead raise the minimum voting age instead of lowering it. With people holding such strong opposition it can be difficult for young people to gain the support needed to enact this change.


The age of majority is a key conflict in this issue also as many believe voting to be a key right one should receive as an adult not before. In the majority of European countries, this is set at the age of 18. Prior to the 1970s, the majority of western countries had set this age of majority at 21. However, following the move to lower the voting age to 18 the age of majority was also lowered. It has remained at this age, as it is usually the age one completes secondary education and leaves home,essentially entering adulthood. While this is a social construct, not a biological one, it remains a strong argument against lowering the voting age. After all, young people below the age of 18 are not considered adults and voting is seen as an adult activity.


There is also the element of disparity in voting trends. Older generations may be apprehensive of the political shift that could occur if young people are included in the electorate and thus may not support teen suffrage. This creates a cycle of the youth not engaging in the vote and the older generations maintaining their unequal political voting power. Equally, there is a divide between regularly engaged activist teens and the less politically engaged youth who are typically less educated and do not live in cities. While the lowered voting age could help bring these young people into the fold, many are wary of the potential political ramifications of this.

Conclusion and Questions to Consider

To conclude, while there are many valid reasons to lower the minimum voting age the proposal faces considerable opposition. Despite the vocal actions of young activists coupled with the longstanding recommendation to lower the voting age, 7 years later only 4 Member States have taken the initiative to do so.


Questions to consider:

  1. Considering the longstanding association of casting a vote with maturity and adulthood, what steps can be taken to placate the worry of young people making ill-informed political decisions?
  2. Following the increase of youth participation in the 2019 European Election what further encouragement can be given to young people to participate in politics?
  3. Seeing as older generations are reluctant to offer more young people the vote, what actions can be taken to ensure young people get their voices heard and opinions considered?
