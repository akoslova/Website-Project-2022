Committee on Culture and Education (CULT)

Edu technology: A significant disruption in the educational systems, caused by the COVID-19 pandemic, expedited the transition to remote learning and drew attention to the role of digital technologies in education. With artificial intelligence (AI) currently high on the political and research agenda, how can the EU rationally prepare its Member States' educational systems for such technological development, balancing both the threats and opportunities surrounding AI?


By Milica Mijatović (RS)

Abstract

Never has there been a more dire need for remote learning to be introduced in schools around Europe than at the beginning of the COVID-19 pandemic. Teachers employed in all types of educational institutions, from primary school to universities, turned to digital platforms for help. As a result, the world discovered that artificial intelligence (AI) systems offer practical support for remote learning and teaching.


The current connection between AI and education in Europe can be categorised into these three areas: the use of AI-powered tools in class, learning about AI, and preparing for AI by making students better understand the impact of AI. Such systems have the potential to offer personalised learning experiences for students while automating some of the teachers’ routine tasks (such as grading). However, while the benefits of AI are promising, the impact of AI on educational systems as well as the future of the interactions between students and teachers in such settings are still ambiguous. Some worry that in remote learning supported by AI, student-teacher interaction becomes obsolete which ultimately has a profound impact on students’ satisfaction and learning outcomes. Others raise concern over the fact that AI teaching systems have the ability to collect and process personal data and sensitive information.

Keywords

  • Remote learning represents a broad term which includes any type of learning that happens outside of a traditional classroom, with the teacher not present in the same location as the students, and which is made possible through technology. Remote learning has therefore paved the way for AI in education during the COVID-19 pandemic when in-person lessons suddenly became too dangerous.
  • Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a term coined in the 1950s that has over time accumulated many different definitions. When talking about remote learning with the help of AI, we refer to machines that have the ability to learn as well as be context-aware.
  • A broader term is the information and communication technologies (ICT) representing the internet-enabled wireless networks as well as older forms of communication technologies such as the landline. When a certain group of people has limited access to ICTs, then a digital divide comes about.
  • Digital divide is described as the gap between individuals who have access to ICTs and those who do not. This divide can be viewed through the lense of gender, social status, as well as physical impairments or the lack thereof.
  • Educational systems encompass different types of educational institutions in a certain country. These institutions function in accordance with both local and international agreements (such as the Bologna Process).

Key Actors and Stakeholders

Education in the European Union (EU) falls under the Union’s supporting competence, meaning that the EU can only intervene to support, coordinate or complement the action of Member States in the field. Despite this, there are several EU institutions and stakeholders working in the area of education. For instance, the goal of the European Education Area (EEA) is to improve the quality of education and training available in Member States, with one of its key areas of improvement being digital education. The European Commission works together with Member States to achieve their collective vision on the EEA. In order to choose and implement the best solutions for education in Europe, the European Education and Culture Executive Agency (EACEA) is in charge of selecting and providing funding for projects in the field of education that benefit all EU citizens.


When faced with the fact that digital technologies have become an essential part of the educational process, both teachers and students alike experience something called a generational digital divide. Students, especially those in high school, grew up learning how to use modern technology, but their teachers lack certain knowledge when it comes to new software or sometimes even hardware. Realising that teachers and students represent the main pillars of education could help make future legislation more people-oriented.


On the EU level, the Directorate-General for Education and Culture (EAC) , is the main department responsible for EU policy on education. It develops and carries out the Commission’s policies and initiatives. At the same time, the Directorate-General for Communications Networks, Content and Technology (CONNECT) develops policies, research, and innovation in the EU with the aim of creating a Europe ‘fit for the digital age’. Above all, Ministries of Education as well as Ministries of Research of respective Member States represent important links between new proposals and their implementation, since Member States reserve the right to organise education and training as they see fit. Bearing in mind that digital technologies are a lucrative area, private companies have a role to play. The Google for Education EU Digital Support Fund is designed to help small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) that support Member States’ national digital education transformation programmes. Google offers SMEs the possibility to apply for funding in order to carry out their programs as well as get Google’s digital equipment and software.

Relevant Policies and Existing Measures

Not many measures that include the link between AI and education exist, however, one of the first documents to mention the urgency of using technology’s potential in education is “ Schools — EU agenda to improve competences and learning for the 21st century”. This paper was drafted in 2008 by the EAC, and it identifies new technologies as an asset to enabling young people to fully develop for their future.


In February of 2021, the European Commission established a framework for setting up six new executive agencies that will be in charge of implementing EU programmes in their respective fields (infrastructure, research, health, education, culture etc.) until 2028. One of which is the European Health and Digital Executive Agency in charge of implementing the Digital Europe Programme (DIGITAL). This programme is designed to provide funding in key areas including AI.


In April of 2021, the Commission proposed the first-ever legal framework on AI called the Artificial Intelligence Act. The aim of this document is to address the risks of AI and ensure that Europeans can trust what AI has to offer them. The Commission's research and innovation policy on artificial intelligence focuses on developing and deploying AI solutions that have positive impacts on the lives of European citizens. At the same time, the Digital Education Action Plan (2021-2027) represents a policy initiative set out to support the sustainable adaptation of the education and training systems of Member States to the digital age. This initiative underlines the importance of having an education system resilient to disruptions such as the COVID-19 pandemic.


Member States have all individually implemented digital learning programmes to make sure children could continue with their education. Some of the most successful examples include Spain’s Educlan platform supported by the Ministry of Education of Spain, France’s Ma classe à la maison or Switzerland’s Eduport website. All of these platforms were created in collaboration with teacher unions and the respective Ministries of Education offering students the chance to access interactive lessons online as well as through Smart TV apps.

Key Challenges

It is believed by some that well-coordinated use of AI can bring about significant improvements to society. It can help the Union reach climate and sustainability goals and introduce high-impact innovations to healthcare, education, transport, industry, and many other sectors. The Commission is determined to make this Europe's “Digital Decade”, when Europe must strengthen its digital sovereignty and set standards.


When focusing on the positives we frequently list advantages of modern technologies, such as having a more interactive learning experience, accessing an unlimited amount of information or acquiring digital literacy. Another essential characteristic of using AI in education is that AI ‘works around the clock’ and never tires, unlike people.



At the same time, the uptake of AI entails a number of potential risks and has the potential to bring about considerable changes to the way we view education. The instantaneous introduction of modern technologies to the classroom due to the COVID-19 pandemic is frequently condemned for having negative consequences, including the widening of an already existing social gap. When education relies on information and communication technologies (ICTs) the digital divide becomes more prominent.


With AI offering student-tailored instruction, some argue that teachers’ roles may shift toward overseers who solely design and select AI-led exercises and monitor student progress, instead of facilitators who lead their own lessons. On the other hand, AI enables teachers and educators of all kinds to better analyse the needs of their students and therefore implement the theory of multiple intelligences (MI) in the way they teach.


Furthermore, teaching methods that solely rely on digital technologies could be connected to the increase in the number of students suffering from mental illness, and the regression of young people’s cognitive and academic development. Aside from these factors, the cost of maintaining modern technology often gets overlooked as well as the ability of AI teaching systems to collect and process sensitive information such as personalised student numbers as well as scanned documents.

The Scientific Background

Artificial intelligence combines computer science and robust datasets to enable problem-solving. AI also encompasses two sub-fields: machine learning and deep learning. Machine learning as a sub-category of AI, focuses on teaching computers how to learn from data and improve with experience. The more data the computer processes, the more it learns and improves its performance. Deep learning relies on neural networks to mimic the behaviour of the human brain. Most common examples of deep learning applications are digital assistants, voice-enabled TV remotes, and credit card fraud detection. These disciplines are comprised of AI algorithms which seek to create expert systems that can make predictions or classifications based on given data.


According to one classification, AI can be split into two groups: weak (narrow) AI and strong AI. Narrow AI is the most common type of AI today. Apple’s built-in assistant Siri is an example of this, along with other automated chat-bots and programmes for automated grading. At the moment, strong AI is only theoretical. When strong or general AI is achieved, machines will have the ability to learn from their past experiences and plan for the future as well as the ability to reason or display common sense, all traits associated only with humans.

Conclusion and Questions to Consider

It seems as though the use of AI will have to become more prevalent and better defined within European education systems with the effects of the COVID-19 pandemic still making it difficult for both teachers and students to participate in “traditional” classes. Moreover, hybrid-learning has been shown to benefit those in need of more flexible learning arrangements due to e.g. illness or family commitments. The main challenge is finding balance in utilising the benefits of AI while still allowing teachers to tailor their lessons and their approach to educating. Another concern relates to both the mental and physical health of teachers and students which comes under risk during remote learning. The digital divide further deepens the social gap that becomes apparent when education relies on ICTs.


Questions to consider:

  1. How can the EU contribute to the development of technologies that assist learning while balancing the benefits and threats of AI?
  2. What role should AI play in the grand scheme of things when it comes to the goals the EU set for its digital development?
  3. How should Member States approach the prospect of digital technologies in education to become better prepared for future disruptions to their education systems?
  4. How does remote learning shape the students’ experience of education and what role does AI play?
  5. How can the EU ensure that every student has the means to access digital educational services?
