Committee on Economic and Monetary Affairs II (ECON II)

Are you WE ready for it?: With the EU directly addressing the disruption of our lives and acknowledging the need for better protection from global hazards through The Recovery and Resilience Facility, the largest component of NextGenerationEU programme, what future steps should the EU and Member States take to increase Europe's resilience and improve the coordination of response to global challenges?


By Vilhelm Hayen (SE)

Abstract

Between 2020-2022, the EU has initiated a number of efforts to mitigate the public health effects and financial repercussions of the COVID-19 pandemic. The European Commission coordinated the joint buying of vaccines and medical equipment for the Member States. The European Central Bank (ECB) robustly increased its purchasing of government and corporate bonds. Likewise, the EU took the extraordinary decision in 2020 to drastically increase the size of its common c apital markets bond issuance programmes, in order to finance the Union’s economic recovery post-pandemic. As a result of the “Corona bonds”, a total of €806.9 billion will be made available to the Member States to finance common goals of climate neutrality, digitalisation, public health resilience, and gender equality. The money is accorded to the Member States through the NextGenerationEU programme and The Recovery and Resilience Facility (RRF). To be eligible for funding, Member States must spend a minimum 37% and 20% of allotted money on climate and digitisation projects respectively. Yet, the EU’s post-Covid-19 recovery programme has been criticised for being both too ambitious and not ambitious enough. Detractors say the issuing of Corona bonds, which encumber all Member States with billions in new government debt regardless of previous economic performance, risk hindering the growth potential of high-productivity countries. Sceptics say the funding appropriated for climate projects is too low, and that the EU is missing a once in a generation opportunity to concretely commit to the Paris Agreement’s goal of limiting global warming to below 2°C.

Keywords

  • The European Semester: A framework through which the Union’s Member states are able to coordinate economic and employment policy. The European Semester likewise serves as a forum for discussions on common fiscal and monetary challenges, and Member States are required to annually report on planned economic reform initiatives.
  • Digital Transition: The transformation of public and private social, financial, and governmental systems from physical to digital, including the operationalising and spread of telemedicine, e-health, and e-governance.
  • NextGenerationEU: The EU’s post-COVID-19 recovery plan, compromising investments of €806.9 billion in the Union's 27 Member States. Through NextGenerationEU, funding is allocated for projects aiming to help the Union achieve climate neutrality by 2050 and foster a faster digital transition. In addition, the European Commission predicts investments made within the framework of NextGenerationEU will contribute to GDP increases of between 1-4% in the Member States.
  • Spillover Effects: A phenomenon within economics which depicts how economic booms, as well as shocks and recessions, in one country inevitably affects other neighbouring countries through knock-on effects. Stemming from close trade ties within the EU and a shared common currency in the Eurozone, economic spillover effects are increasingly common between the Member States.
  • International Risk Sharing: Describes government led efforts to safeguard consumption levels in times of economic shock and financial crisis. In the event domestic consumption drops, “consumption insurance” through international risk sharing allows states the ability to invest in and borrow from other states. This in turn helps preserve consumption flows, and limits the effects of economic recession on the national economy.

Key Actors and Stakeholders

The European Commission, led by President Ursula von der Leyen, has been a vocal supporter throughout the COVID-19 pandemic of further EU cooperation and coordination. In consultation with the European Council, the Commission brought the RRF to life in February 2021, ensuring joint Union-wide financing for post-pandemic reforms in the Member States. The von der Leyen Commission has likewise argued the need for European unity to meet major continental and global challenges and crises, including climate change and the Russian invasion of Ukraine. The Treaty of Lisbon clarifies the areas in which the EU – and European Commission – retains the competency to act and propose legislation.


The European Central Bank (ECB) used non-standard policy measures throughout the COVID-19 pandemic to combat the risk of economic depression and extensive unemployment, including engaging in large-scale quantitative easing (QE). Under its’ €1850 billion pandemic emergency purchase programme (PEPP), the ECB bought a myriad of assets from banks, companies and governments to ensure liquidity and stability in the European financial system. The ECB’s use of non-standard policy measures during the COVID-19 pandemic, as throughout the financial crisis of 2008-2009 and Eurozone debt crisis of 2010-2013, provides European capital markets with additional cash and credit assets to mitigate the effects of financial recession and negate broad price fluctuations.


Directorate-General for European Civil Protection and Humanitarian Aid Operations (DG ECHO) serves as the European Commission’s organ for the deliverance of relief aid to populations affected by natural disasters and man-made crises. DG ECHO directs a European and global network of field offices and is the body responsible for carrying out operations under the EU Civil Protection Mechanism to aid countries in need of emergency relief assistance. DG ECHO is currently involved in coordinating the distribution of material aid to Ukraine following the Russian invasion of the country in February 2022.


Member States’ governments are responsible for the implementation and execution of national response plans in times of crisis. In regards to COVID-19, Member States’ governments faced the decision on how best respond to the pandemic, with some opting for nationwide lockdowns while others chose more relaxed public health measures, such as temporarily limiting public gatherings and recommending social distancing. To receive NextGenerationEU funding, Member State governments are required to adhere to a number of commonly agreed criteria , and send in national plans detailing how they aim to spend their allocated recovery funds. A number of Member State governments, including France and Germany, have supported the creation of new political tools to better coordinate future EU crisis management.

Relevant Policies and Existing Measures

Core to the changing discourse on European financial cooperation and integration is the newfound acceptance of taking on common debt in the Union through the selling of joint bonds. On behalf of the EU, the European Commission is committed to borrowing a minimum of €750 billion on international capital markets to finance post-pandemic recovery reforms. While not a unique arrangement, pre-pandemic outstanding common bond debt amounted to a total of €52 billion, less than a tenth the size of the NextGenerationEU programme. Member States gain access to NextGenerationEU funding through the RRF, which delivers approved capital on the basis of submitted national plans. The plans must include measures to “advance green and digital transitions and make Member States’ economies and societies more resilient.” Funding is continuously approved by the RRF. Member States are able to consecutively be permitted NextGenerationEU resources up until 2026, should the current RRF mandate not be prolonged. RRF funding is further performance-based, and regular payments require recipient states to adhere to their target milestones and make efforts to achieve country-specific fiscal and policy recommendations set out by the European Semester. All Member States, except the Netherlands, have so far submitted national plans which have received approval from the European Commission.


While current focus lies on a resilient post-pandemic rebuilding effort, other policies and measures exist to meet separate types of global hazards. As extreme weather, environmental degradation and sea-level rise increases as a consequence of the climate crisis, the EU regularly prepares overviews of natural and human-induced disaster risks to practically model necessary changes in risk management policy. Through the EU Civil Protection Mechanism , a DG ECHO responsibility area, 33 states (including all EU countries and 6 additional participating nations) exchange information on estimates of current and future disaster risk. The EU Civil Protection Mechanism in like manner provides funding for projects in the Member States aiming to heighten disaster risk prevention capabilities.

Key Challenges

The EU continues to suffer from institutional obstacles in pursuing a coherent and concerted response to global challenges. Yet change seems to be the theme of the future; the introduction of comprehensive common debt, which the European Commissions aims to repay by 2058 the earliest, and mechanisms to bypass the once believed impassable necessity of unanimity in decision-making signal giants move too, albeit slowly. Still, key to creating sustainable and vigorous answers to future global hazards and disasters for the Union will be observing whether or not the current winds of change are temporary. Likewise, it remains to be seen if Member States receiving RRF funding continue to comply with targets and reforms advancing climate-friendly and digitisation efforts in the years to come. While the European Commission and Member States’ governments claim 40% of NextGenerationEU grants and loans will go towards a green transition, NGOs are sceptical.


Further, the European Central Bank is still providing colossal economic support to the countries of the Economic and Monetary Union (EMU), with most central banks in Member States outside the Eurozone engaged in similar ongoing non-standard policy measures. All Member States have throughout the pandemic similarly employed vast spending programmes and extended deficit expenditure to hinder job losses and wage decreases, with some continuing the programmes into 2022 and 2023. In response to the expansion of financial relief, inflation throughout the EU has increased to levels not noted in decades – consequently, worries may arise as to the viability of lasting widespread monetary and fiscal stimulus as a catalyst for economic growth.

Ideologies and Schools of Thought

As is custom on most issues of major importance in the European discourse, clashes exist between those who believe further EU integration and collaboration paves the way for common prosperity, and those who believe supranational interference in sovereign nation states should be minimised. Schisms often materialise between net contributors and net beneficiaries of EU funds. Geographically, differences on financial policy tend to emerge along a Northern and Southern divide. In passing the Multiannual Financial Framework (MFF) – the EU’s long-term budget – for 2021–2027, of which NextGenerationEU is part, debate arose as to the size of the Union’s collective spending to help Member States rebuild and recover post-pandemic. While Italy, Greece, Bulgaria, and Hungary sought to expand the proportion of recovery funds allocated as grants, Austria, Denmark, Sweden, and the Netherlands (a group known as the “frugal four”) instead argued a majority of funding should come in the form of loans – to be paid back by the Member States which received them. NextGenerationEU spending was eventually agreed to be equally split, with 50% of funds assigned as grants, and 50% assigned as loans. Yet, with the passing of the MFF came another question: how should the EU ascertain that, as it prepared its’ largest budgetary programme ever, Union funding did not become complicit in infringements on democracy, human security, and civil liberties? A protracted and controversial issue, the answer elected was the implementation of a “rule of law conditionality regulation”. Since 2021, the EU has been able to withhold funding to Member States, should the Union believe actions taken by any one country places EU financial interests in danger. Supporters of the regulation contend the measure will deter governments from trying to dismantle democratic institutions, strengthen adherence to the EU’s common values and provide a stronger framework for meeting European and global challenges. Critics claim conditioning funding will only hurt already marginalised groups in poorer states, while doing little to dissuade governments from taking financially erroneous decisions and engaging in anti-democratic behaviour. In staunch opposition to the conditionality regulation stand Poland and Hungary, of which the former has already halted planned development programmes dependent on EU funding in response to the European Court of Justice’s ruling in February 2022 on the issue; the Court in its decision stated that the introduction of a rule-of-law mechanism "was adopted on an appropriate legal basis".

Conclusion and Questions to Consider

How the EU continues to engage with the great threats of our time – COVID-19, the Russo-Ukrainian war, and climate change – will play a large part in determining Europe’s role in the world and the future direction of the Union. While the European Commission and Member States have largely touted the passing of NextGenerationEU as a considerable success, questions remain as to whether the adoption of the RRF and MFF 2021–2027 will truly be a watershed moment for the EU – pointing the way towards deeper future European cooperation. Still, the von der Leyen Commission has reason to be sanguine: NextGenerationEU funds have begun arriving in Member States, green energy is increasingly replacing fossil fuels in the European energy mix, and the EU has shown unanticipated unity in enacting sanctions on those responsible for the Russian invasion of Ukraine. Yet, hazards lie in the future. The embracement of unprecedented common debt in the EU risks placing additional stress on government finances in Member States with high debt-to-GDP ratios. To tackle inflation worries, the ECB and European central banks alike have signalled likely interest rate increases coming in 2022 and 2023. Such moves would hike interest expenditure and cause a jump in debt financing costs for the Member States at a time when average EU national debt is at an all-time high. With the ECB expected to scale back its usage of QE by the end of 2023, a number of highly indebted Member States may subsequently need to enforce strategies of fiscal consolidation and austerity programmes to avoid financial instability and a loss of investor confidence.


Questions to consider:

  1. With climate change predicted to escalate the chance of new disease outbreaks, what should the EU do to assure it is financially able to support Member States in the case of a new pandemic?
  2. How should the EU work to ensure financial stability in the Union as the ECB ends its use of non-standard monetary policy measures?
  3. The window of discourse in European politics has shifted throughout the COVID-19 pandemic; should the EU continue seeking deeper integration to tackle global challenges, or are Member States themselves better suited to meeting the needs of their citizens in times of crisis?
  4. Do conditioning EU funding on compliance with rule of law regulations and policy goals help or hinder Member States in pursuing economic development and shock resilience?
