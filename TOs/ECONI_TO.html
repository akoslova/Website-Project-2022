Committee on Economic and Monetary Affairs I (ECON I)

What goes around, comes around: The European Commission adopted the new Circular Economy Action Plan (CEAP) in 2020 as a part of the European Green Deal. Bearing in mind that the transition towards a circular economy is necessary for the EU to reach its 2050 climate neutrality target, how can the EU move forward from a linear model of production and consumption toward a more sustainable future?


By Joris Dietz (NL)

Abstract

Currently, the capitalist and western European society maintains a linear model for producing and consuming goods. Suppliers collect and process raw materials from the earth, manufacturers build components to assemble into products, which are marketed and distributed by retailers, to be used and eventually discarded by consumers. Consequently, value and energy are taken from but not returned to nature, but lost in waste and emissions.


Experts agree that our linear economic model is unsustainable and will have to change. In order to secure revenue in the long-term, companies need to act now to change the way they do business and make a pivot to circularity. This model aims to refuse, reduce, reuse, repair, and recycle products and materials in order to save natural resources. For this people need to rethink and redesign their ways of working and living. Here, (virtual) services come in, closing the loops and adding extra value to consumer’s experiences. The EU can be innovative and should drive this transformation, and thus, concrete policies and programs on circularity are needed.


Main challenges revolve around conflicts with both consumers and producers while political powers seem insufficiently effective in their initiatives. It is crucial to understand the concepts of sustainability and innovation and how circular economy and society differ. Then, various approaches can be found to address the current issues and move forward in the transition to circularity. A circular economy is more than just a move towards waste reduction and increased recycling. It is a paradigm shift – a new way of thinking about economic activity - that is needed to completely reshape our current economic model at a global level.

Keywords

  • The Circular Economy is a systems solution framework that tackles global challenges like climate change, biodiversity loss, waste, and pollution. The concept is based on three principles, driven by design: Eliminate waste and pollution, circulate products and materials (at their highest value), and regenerate nature. It is underpinned by a transition to renewable energy and materials. A circular economy decouples economic activity from the consumption of finite resources. It is a resilient system that is good for business, people, and the environment.
  • Sustainability is an absolute term to indicate a material’s or organism’s ability to sustain itself, i.e. to keep existing. Sustainable development is abstractly defined as development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. The Triple Bottom Line is a framework to plan and evaluate sustainable development in the domain of People, Planet, and Prosperity.
  • The law of supply and demand is a theory that explains the interaction between the sellers of an economic good and the buyers for that good. It defines the relationship between the price of a given product or service and the willingness of people to either buy or sell it. Generally, as price increases, people are willing to supply more and demand less and vice versa when the price falls.
  • Innovation is a word used to describe a vast number of changes to a firm’s activities that lead to improved firm performance. These changes can relate to new or improved products or processes, investment in new machines, marketing expenditures, investment in training, the creation of intellectual property, or the purchase of technology. The multifaceted nature of innovation makes a concise measure of innovation, which is appropriate to all firms, impossible. Various types and models of innovation have been developed, like the Ansoff matrix.

Key Actors and Stakeholders

The European Commission’s Directorate‑General for Economic and Financial Affairs (DG ECFIN) is responsible for EU policies promoting economic growth and recovery, higher employment, stable public finances, and financial stability. This department is tasked to, among others, provide economic analysis and an evaluation of policy areas of the European Union such as the environment.


The European Commission’s Directorate-General for Environment (DG ENV) is the European authority on environmental policies. This department proposes and implements policies that ensure a high level of environmental protection and preserve the quality of life of EU citizens. Through shared competence between the European Commission and Member States, the DG ENV is powered to legislate and adopt legally binding acts in the area of circular economy.


The United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) is the global authority and advocate on the environmental agenda. It includes a knowledge platform with research publications and a Partnership for Action on Green Economy, to advise and guide countries, the private sector and civil society on their paths to a green and inclusive economy.


The European Investment Bank is the ‘lending arm’ of the European Union and contributes to the circular economy by providing financing, advisory support, and knowledge sharing to circular economy projects/promoters, including those with a higher risk profile.


Companies are the driving force in the economy as suppliers, producers, and retailers of products and materials. Industries have the potential and power to transition to the circular economy, but also to maintain the linear model.


Cities have a high concentration of resources, capital, data, and talent spread over a relatively small geographic area and are centres for innovation. By 2050 two thirds of the population will live in cities, consuming 75% of the world’s natural resources, producing 50% of global waste and over 60% of greenhouse gas emissions.


Citizens are the receiving end in the economy as customers, consumers, and users of products and materials. Markets evolve around changing needs, supply, and demand and so also have the potential and power to determine the economic model.

Relevant Policies and Existing Measures

The Circular Economy Action Plan is part of the European Green Deal and the ambitious follow-up to a strategy whose goals had been met. Two years after its implementation in 2020, consumer home appliances and smart electronics have been redesigned for repair (although printers remain unregulated). Plans for green batteries and textile and measures against greenwashing are still on the drawing board. Yet, the European Commission failed to adequately address waste prevention and recycling, particularly for the built environment.


EU laws on waste - most prominently the Waste Framework Directive and the Landfill Directive - tackle all types of waste with specified directives. The 2019 Single Use Plastic Directive is a recent example of regulation that will target, and in some cases ban, the ten single-use plastic products most often found on Europe’s beaches and in its seas.


Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) is another increasingly popular approach to making the polluter pay. It makes producers responsible for the treatment and/or disposal of post-consumer products (financially by paying for or physically by collecting, treating, and disposing of the products). EPR measures incentivise the reduction of waste at its source. Today, many governments have EPR policies in place and the trend is towards an extension of EPR to new product groups and waste streams, such as electrical appliances and electronics.


Initiatives to empower consumers that drive the circular economy are widespread over Europe and range from DIY plastic recycling kits to webinars about greenwashing in the textile industry.

Key Challenges

The mainstream approach to circular economy is capital centric, i.e. focused in production on the return of financial capital. Most policy discourses and innovations are meant to make capitalism more efficient, more lean, and more sustainable and find technological fixes around the surpluses, excesses, and contradictions of capitalist development. For the circular economy to succeed a design thinking approach needs to be adopted, where meaningful virtual services complement or obviate physical products.


Product-as-a-service models often work better in the B2B market than in the B2C market, because circular products and services suffer from a negative image. Consumers consider reused or refurbished products to be inferior when compared with new products. They have a misperception that circular products and services are of lower quality or more expensive than traditional products and services. Incentives for consumers to repair a product instead of buying a new one are generally weak. Although they are seeking more sustainable brands, with green considerations playing a stronger role in decision-making, greenwashing disguises the ongoing unsustainable reality.


In the modern world, many companies find that their business model is so interconnected with those of other companies that they are unable to act independently in their transformation to the circular economy. Yet, businesses suffer from a lack of cooperation of multiple companies along the supply chain due to their different interests and priorities. Also there is a limited transparency on the origin or content of products, resources, and materials, which may discourage companies from adopting circular practices.


Measures like EPR and waste directives pressure governments and companies to limit and better process their waste. Yet, European waste processing statistics are misleading. The problem of massive piles of waste is simply moved out of sight, out of mind. Waste leaks from Europe to Africa through illegal routes, where there's no proper facility to actually manage it. The circular potential of mostly e-waste is lost and human health is harmed.

Ideologies and Schools of Thought

A circular society defines discourses with a vision of circularity where not only resources are circulated in sustainable loops, but also wealth, knowledge, technology, and power is circulated and redistributed throughout society. These discourses thus comprehensively include the three pillars of sustainability and see circularity as a holistic transition, where issues of political empowerment and social justice also have to be addressed. In contrast, the term circular economy focuses on circulating resources alone and deals with circularity through a technical lens of ecological and material efficiency alone.


In social (gamma) studies, two approaches to information processing exist that can be applied to economics, but also politics and design. Moving from a linear to a circular economy requires both a top-down and a bottom-up approach. By top-down, we refer to policies that discourage linearity (such as landfill tax) and also support circularity. These policies are enacted through regulatory levers only governments can control. By bottom-up, we mean the recognition, by companies, of the financial benefits that resource-efficient, circular, business models can bring, and to then work with the waste management sector to deliver these benefits. From a broader perspective, also users and material suppliers in the product life cycle could call for circularity.

Conclusion and Questions to Consider

The European Union (EU) has taken a particular approach to circularity, with high expectations to increase competitiveness, promote economic growth, and create jobs while reducing environmental impacts and resource dependency. However, the circular economy is a contested paradigm, for which many competing interpretations exist, each seeking varying degrees of social, ecological, and political transformation. Ultimately, political, business, and consumer powers experiment with and apply various approaches in the short term to make impact on the long run. Conflicting interests and complex systems are challenging the abundance of initiatives and so thorough research and constructive conversation are needed on the road to circularity.


Questions to consider:

  1. Who is responsible for the life cycle of products?
  2. How can people change their behaviour and demand to incite the circular economy from a bottom-up approach?
  3. What needs to change in supply chains to ensure a circular economy?
  4. When is the ultimate deadline for a circular economy?
  5. Consider your own consumption behaviour and note 5 products or services that could be more circular.
