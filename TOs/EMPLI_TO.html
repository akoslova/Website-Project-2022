Committee on Employment and Social Affairs I (EMPL I)

Home, cheap home: Irish taoiseach Micheál Martin called housing the “most urgent social issue facing our country” but all across Europe, from Dublin to Athens, more and more people struggle to afford the rising costs of housing, especially young people. How can the EU ensure access to decent and affordable housing as a human right for all Europeans?


By Yannika Rönnqvist (FI/PL)

Abstract

Europe is in the midst of a housing crisis with Europeans spending an increasing amount of their disposable income on housing. In 2021, one in ten Europeans spent roughly 40% of their income on housing. Simultaneously, government spending on affordable and social housing has significantly decreased in most EU Member States, resulting in housing inequality and widespread homelessness. In just the last decade the level of homelessness in the EU increased by a whopping 70%, not counting informal homelessness such as “sofa surfing” and rough sleeping.


Students and young people in general belong to one of the most vulnerable g roups which has given rise to the so-called “boomerang generation”. This affects 80% of young people aged 18 to 24 in Europe who are unable to settle down permanently. This is caused by a number of things, including social housing shortages, inflated rent and property prices, as well as the commodification of the housing market. These problems have been further exacerbated by the ongoing Covid-19 pandemic as an increasing number of people are facing financial issues and the construction sector has been greatly impeded. Moreover, many universities across Europe have decided to limit the availability of campus accommodation for students to avoid overcrowding.


Housing, in particular for young people, is an increasing problem in Europe, however, not all countries are affected equally or have the same measures in place to combat the issue. For instance, the Netherlands, Sweden, and the UK have the biggest social renter sector, while countries such as Portugal, Spain, and Greece are characterised by a large privately owned housing sector.

Keywords

  • Housing inequality is a form of social inequality that occurs when citizens experience a disparity in the quality of housing.
  • Social housing is any rental housing owned and managed by the state, local authorities, or non-profit organisations. Social housing aims to provide a more affordable option to housing than for example private actors.
  • Informal homelessness occurs when people experience homelessness without being in contact with any homelessness services, thus being difficult to count and include in statistics. This includes “sofa surfing” and rough sleeping.
  • Booomerang generation is the term used to describe young people aged 18 to 24 that still live with their parents, frequently moving in and out without being able to permanently settle down.
  • Financialisation of housing occurs when housing is treated as a commodity rather than a social good.

Key Actors and Stakeholders

The European Parliament is the legislative body of the European Union (EU). Some of its key responsibilities include the supervision of EU institutions and the budget of the Union. Along with the European Commission , which initiates legislation to be approved by the European Parliament, it has the power to allocate funding to projects and initiatives that can alleviate the current housing crisis in the EU as well as support its 27 Member States.


Although the European Parliament and Commission can support the EU Member States in combating the housing crisis, the EU holds no direct competence in housing policy. Thus, it is up to each of its 27 Member States to individually legislate, making them key players in solving the issue. The Member States, in particular cities and local governments, have the possibility to create policies on housing as well as fund and organise measures that can help relieve the problem, such as social housing. The EU is, however, not entirely powerless in combating the European housing crisis. Through schemes such as The Affordable Housing Initiative, which is part of the Commission's renovation wave strategy for Europe, the EU can help combat the issue.


Non-governmental organisations (NGOs) and non-profit organisations (NPOs) are both important players in combating Europe’s housing crisis. They can provide citizens with social and affordable housing, as well as lobby decision makers to improve financial support services and create new policies or reform existing ones on the issue. NGOs such as the European Students’ Union (ESU) and the West European Student Information Bureau (WESIB) are concerned with student housing in particular.


European citizens, in particular young people and students, are the actors directly affected by the problem. A lack of affordable housing and measures to combat the problem subject young people to homelessness (formal and informal), debt, and prevent them from moving away from their parents. European citizens along with large scale investors (both foreign and European) are also a part of the housing shortage and rapid increase in prices of rents and properties. Both actors contribute to the problem by investing in property that is then either rented out on the private housing market or through short term rental sites, effectively commodifying housing.

Relevant Policies and Existing Measures

The right to adequate housing is a human right recognised in international human rights law. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR), article 25 (1) states that “Everyone has the right to a standard of living adequate for the health and well-being of himself and of his family, including food, clothing, housing […]”. Furthermore, the Charter of Fundamental Rights of the European Union includes adequate housing as one of the most important rights in the EU in article 34 (3).


The European Parliament recently passed a resolution on decent and affordable housing for all. In the report the European Parliament recommends a number of measures to the European Commission and Member States, such as an increase in European Structural Investment (ESI) Funds to guarantee accessible and affordable housing, as well as the establishment of an Integrated Strategy on public, social, and affordable housing. Moreover, a number of EU initiatives make funding available for mitigating the housing crisis. For instance, the EU cohesion policy of 377 billion euros will invest in social housing, sustainable neighbourhoods, as well as other basic services. Furthermore, the new InvestEU will support social student housing in the EU with 16.6 billion euros.


However, housing and housing rights are not formally a European Union competence, and it is largely up to each Member State to implement measures to combat the housing crisis. As these measures are taken on a national level, the policies implemented vary from country to country, resulting in a fragmented housing policy landscape across the EU. During the Covid-19 pandemic, most EU countries implemented short term emergency measures to support the most vulnerable households. These policies have for the most part been temporary and many countries fail to implement long term policies combating the European housing crisis.

Key Challenges

One of the biggest challenges with the current European housing crisis is the commodification and financialisation of the housing market in Europe, and the rising housing inequality ensuing from this. Owning property is seen as an increasingly profitable asset in Europe and many people as well as large scale investors, often foreign investors such as the private equity company Blackstone, choose to invest in multiple properties to either rent out on the private housing market or through short term rental sites such as Airbnb. This drives up the prices of rentals on the private market and forces young people and students to compete for housing with tourists. For instance, the average income of a young person in Lisbon was 900 euros per month in 2021, while the average price for a studio was 1000 euros.


While housing prices keep increasing on the private market, Europe is experiencing a shortage of affordable and social housing. The investment gap in the social housing sector is estimated to be 57 billion euros per year in the EU and most Member States have consistently decreased their spending on social housing in the last decade. Furthermore, students face an increasing problem with finding affordable housing, fueled by the rising demand for studying abroad. In the Netherlands alone 22 000 students were unable to find student accommodation in 2020, a number that is expected to double over the next few years. This forces students to look towards the private market or other alternative housing options such as hotels, which tend to be significantly less affordable.

The Past and the Future

Many of the housing problems Europe is facing today stem from the financial crisis in 2008. As millions of Europeans faced evictions, investors saw their chance to invest in the properties left behind, ultimately shifting housing to be regarded as a commodity rather than a social good. Another consequence of the financial crisis in 2008 was that many countries decided to cut funding for social housing. For instance, Ireland cut their spending on social housing by 72 percent between 2008–2012.


Fast forward to present day Europe. Since the Russian invasion on Ukraine on February 24, 2022, over 500 000 Ukrainians have already fled the country (February 28, 2022) and as the Russian aggression is picking up pace, more are expected to come. Most refugees tend to flee to the countries neighbouring Ukraine such as Poland, Moldova, Hungary, Romania, and Slovakia. Poland, for instance, reported that as many as 100 000 Ukrainians crossed the Polish-Ukrainian border within the first 48 hours. With the housing crisis already being a pressing problem in Europe, how will Europe, in particular the countries neighbouring Ukraine, respond to the increasing number of Ukrainian refugees being forced to leave Ukraine? This question opens up a broader conversation about the treatment of migrants and their right to adequate housing, especially in the context of the recent European migrant crisis in 2015.

Conclusion and Questions to Consider

The current housing crisis is undoubtedly a pressing problem in Europe, affecting young people and students in particular. Some of the key challenges include the lack of affordable and social housing as well as financialisation of the housing market. Access to adequate housing is a recognised human right in international law, as well as by the EU, yet the rates of housing inequality does not seem to decrease, rather the opposite.


Questions to consider:

  1. What can the EU and its 27 Member States do to guarantee the “boomerang generation” adequate housing, prevent housing inequality, and informal homelessness?
  2. How can the EU and its 27 Member States incentivise local governments and NPOs to invest in social and affordable housing?
  3. How can the EU and its 27 Member States prevent and limit the implications of the financialisation of the housing market?
